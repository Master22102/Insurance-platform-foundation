1. Extracted Context from Prior Discussions
Prior discussions established that travelers frequently hold multiple overlapping forms of protection, including airline obligations, credit card benefits, travel insurance, and sometimes home or renter’s insurance. When disruptions occur, users often act under stress and file claims without understanding sequencing, primacy, or downstream effects.
Key risks identified:
* Filing the wrong claim first can trigger denial cascades due to secondary coverage clauses or “other insurance” language.

* Accepting airline compensation (refunds, vouchers, credits, rebooking) can change how other providers later evaluate the loss.

* Premium, eligibility, or relationship consequences may arise when filing small or unnecessary claims against long-term policies.

* In some cases, waiting, documenting, or not filing at all may preserve more options than immediate action.

* Airline resolutions accepted too early may unintentionally limit credit card or insurance benefits.

The platform’s role is to slow users down, surface tradeoffs, and make consequences legible — without guaranteeing outcomes or providing legal advice.
________________


2. Purpose
To help travelers understand how and when to route claims across airlines, credit cards, insurers, and long-term policies in order to reduce avoidable denials, preserve eligibility, and minimize friction.
This feature provides decision support only. It does not submit claims, determine outcomes, or replace provider judgment.

7. Decision Logic (plain language, no code)
The engine evaluates claim routing through the following steps:
7.1 Acceptance Checkpoint (Mandatory)
The system explicitly asks whether the user has already accepted any of the following:
                                          * Refund

                                          * Rebooking

                                          * Voucher

                                          * Credit

                                          * Partial reimbursement or goodwill compensation

If yes, routing logic adjusts immediately.
Acceptance does not automatically bar further benefits, but it changes how the loss is evaluated later. Other providers may consider accepted compensation when determining eligibility or reimbursement amounts.
________________


7.2 Cause Classification
The incident is classified as one of the following:
                                             * Clear airline responsibility

                                             * Shared or ambiguous cause

                                             * External or systemic cause

                                             * User- or itinerary-driven cause

Cause classification determines confidence level and language framing.
________________


7.3 Coverage Layer Evaluation
The system identifies:
                                                * Airline obligations and remedies

                                                * Credit card benefit applicability

                                                * Travel insurance coverage and exclusions

                                                * Long-term policy relevance (home/renter)

Primary vs secondary language and dependency clauses are surfaced.
________________


7.4 Small Claim Warning Band
When all of the following apply:
                                                   * Claim value is relatively small

                                                   * Target policy has long-term consequences

                                                   * Alternative resolution paths exist

The system presents a cautionary framing:
“This claim appears relatively small.
Some policies treat any claim as part of your long-term history.
You may want to weigh the value of this claim against potential future eligibility or premium considerations.”
No recommendation is made.
________________


7.5 User-Initiated Action Forks
If the user does not want to wait for provider resolution, the system presents choice-forward options:
Example:
“If you don’t want to wait for airline resolution, you may arrange your own transportation.
This may affect reimbursement depending on provider rules.
What would you like to do?”
The system explains consequences without blocking action.
7.6 Guidance Posture & Confidence Framing
The system does not present guidance as binary “covered” or “not covered,” nor does it defer interpretive responsibility back to the user. Instead, it frames guidance using graduated guidance postures, reflecting both policy language and real-world procedural realities.
This framing is designed to be especially useful when users are operating under stress, time constraints, or outside carrier business hours.
Direct Guidance
Used when policy language, incident classification, and typical carrier procedures align clearly.
The system provides a straightforward recommended path.
Expected coverage boundaries are explained in plain language.
Documentation requirements are explicitly listed and queued.
Where applicable, the system identifies the correct carrier or provider reporting channel (e.g., specific disruption or verification portals), rather than generic contact links.
Language example:
“Based on the policy language and the event described, this expense is typically eligible up to the stated limit. Here’s what to document and where to report it when you’re ready.”
Conditional Guidance
Used when coverage applicability depends on specific conditions such as timing thresholds, sequencing choices, or overlapping policies.
The system explains why multiple paths exist.
Tradeoffs are clearly described.
The user is not told to “wait and see,” but is guided on how to preserve options.
Documentation is proactively requested, not deferred.
Language example:
“Coverage here depends on how this event is classified by the carrier. To protect your options, we recommend capturing the following documentation now. You can decide on the filing sequence once this is in place.”
Protected Guidance
Used when external variables (e.g., jurisdictional differences, carrier discretion, or conflicting policy language) materially affect outcomes.
The system still performs interpretive analysis and explains what the policy language supports.
Uncertainty is framed around external acceptance, not internal understanding.
The system emphasizes safeguarding the user’s position by:
documenting thoroughly
avoiding irreversible actions prematurely
Carrier-specific reporting procedures are highlighted when known.
Language example:
“The policy language supports coverage for this type of expense, but outcomes can vary based on how the carrier classifies the event. To protect your position, here’s exactly what to document and the appropriate channel to report through.”
General Principles
Guidance posture affects language, pacing, and emphasis, not user autonomy.
The system never withholds interpretation; it contextualizes it.
Documentation is always framed as a proactive safeguard, not a delay tactic.
The platform explicitly acknowledges that users may be operating outside business hours and designs guidance accordingly.

8. Failure Modes & Safe Defaults
When certainty is low or information is incomplete, the system:
                                                         * Defaults to transparency over confidence

                                                         * Explicitly states uncertainty

                                                         * Encourages documentation before filing

                                                         * Treats waiting as a valid outcome

                                                         * Avoids ranking options as “best” or “correct”

                                                         * Never pressures immediate filing

Acceptance-related uncertainty is framed as evaluation change, not prohibition.
